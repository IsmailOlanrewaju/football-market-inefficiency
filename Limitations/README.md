## Limitations

### Position-Specific Metrics Simplify Player Contributions

To maintain comparability across positions and reduce modelling bias, each model focused primarily on the core responsibilities associated with a player's role:

- Defenders: defensive actions (interceptions, recoveries, tackles won)
- Midfielders: ball-winning and creative contributions
- Forwards: attacking output metrics

This approach improves fairness within positions but does not capture every aspect of player value.

For example, a defender may command a high transfer fee because of exceptional ball progression, leadership, tactical versatility, or goal contributions. However, including attacking metrics for defenders could unfairly advantage a small subset of players and distort comparisons with defenders whose primary responsibility is defensive stability.

As a result, the models should be interpreted as estimates of value based on core positional performance rather than complete measures of player quality.

---

### U-23 Players Represent a Unique Market Segment

This study focuses exclusively on U-23 players from Europe's top five leagues.

Unlike established professionals, younger players are often valued not only for what they currently produce but also for what clubs believe they may become in the future.

Consequently, transfer values in this age group frequently reflect projected potential, developmental upside, and long-term expectations that cannot be fully captured by current-season performance statistics.

The findings should therefore be interpreted as a case study of how the modern market values young talent rather than a universal valuation model for all footballers.

---

### The Challenge of Measuring Football Value

One of the most important findings of this project is that current football valuation systems remain difficult to explain using measurable performance alone.

Across all three position-specific models, only 25%–32% of market value variation was explained by the selected performance metrics.

This raises a broader question:

> Should football transfer values be driven primarily by measurable performance, or by factors such as club reputation, market narratives, and future expectations?

The results suggest that current transfer markets place significant weight on factors that extend beyond observable on-field output.

While future potential is undoubtedly valuable, the findings indicate that football still lacks a transparent framework for combining performance data with these additional valuation drivers.

---

### Statistical Assumptions

Diagnostic testing indicated:

- Low multicollinearity across all models
- Acceptable homoscedasticity
- Generally stable coefficient estimates

However, the forward model showed departures from residual normality, suggesting that some valuation patterns remain unexplained.

Given the complexity of football markets, predictions should therefore be interpreted as informed statistical estimates rather than definitive measures of player worth.

As football analytics continues to evolve, the next challenge will be developing valuation frameworks that combine traditional performance metrics with broader indicators of potential, tactical value, development trajectory, and long-term impact.

Ultimately, the future of football valuation may depend on finding a balance between what players have demonstrated on the pitch and what clubs believe they can become.
